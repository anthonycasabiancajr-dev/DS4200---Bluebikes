Boston Blue Bikes: Ridership Trends Before and After COVID-19

Introduction

The COVID-19 pandemic disrupted urban transportation systems across the country, and Boston's Blue Bikes bike share program was no exception. Lockdowns, remote work, and shifting commuting patterns altered who was riding, when, and for how long. This project examines Blue Bikes ridership data from 2019 (pre-pandemic) and 2022–2023 (post-pandemic recovery) to understand how usage patterns changed — and whether ridership has returned to its pre-pandemic character.

Three dimensions are explored: overall ridership volume over time, the balance between subscriber (annual member) and casual (single-ride or day-pass) riders, when and where rides occur, and how long trips last. Together, these views reveal a system that has grown substantially in total trips but shifted meaningfully in who is using it and how.

About the Data

This analysis uses publicly available trip data from Blue Bikes System Data. Three full calendar years are compared: 2019, 2022, and 2023. Each row in the raw data represents a single trip and includes the following fields:

VariableDescription
tripdurationLength of the trip in seconds
starttime / stoptimeTimestamp when the trip began and ended
start station / end stationName and ID of the origin and destination docking station
start station latitude/longitudeGeographic coordinates of the origin station
usertypeSubscriber (annual member) or Customer (casual rider)
birth yearRider's birth year (available for subscribers)
genderRider's self-reported gender (available for subscribers)

Trips shorter than 1 minute or longer than 3 hours were excluded as likely data errors or dock adjustments. The 2020 and 2021 data were excluded due to pandemic-related disruptions that make those years difficult to interpret as either a stable baseline or a clean recovery period.

Visualization 1: Monthly Ridership Over Time

Total trip counts by month reveal the strong seasonal pattern that characterizes urban bike share — ridership climbs sharply through spring and summer, then falls off in fall and winter. Comparing the three years shows that 2022 and 2023 both substantially exceed 2019 in total volume, particularly in the late-summer and fall months. The post-pandemic system is not merely recovered; it is larger.

One notable shift is the flattening of the winter trough. January and February 2023 saw nearly twice the ridership of those same months in 2019, suggesting a broader and more committed rider base has formed — possibly driven by the expansion of the docking station network or increased adoption of e-bikes.

Visualization 4: Annual Trips by Rider Type

Blue Bikes distinguishes between subscribers (annual members) and customers (casual riders who purchase single-ride or day passes). In 2019, subscribers made up roughly 79% of all trips. By 2022 and 2023, that share had declined slightly — casual riders now account for approximately 24–26% of annual trips, up from 21% before the pandemic.

In absolute terms, both groups grew. Casual ridership nearly doubled from 2019 to 2022, which may reflect growth in tourism, recreational cycling, or new riders trying the system without committing to a membership. Subscriber trip totals also grew substantially, indicating that the core user base expanded alongside the casual segment.

Summary

Taken together, these visualizations paint a picture of a bike share system in transition. Total ridership has grown well beyond pre-pandemic levels, driven by both a larger subscriber base and a notable increase in casual usage. The timing of rides still reflects commuter behavior on weekdays, but the growth in weekend and off-peak ridership suggests that Blue Bikes is reaching a broader range of use cases — recreation, errands, and tourism — than it did in 2019.

Future analysis could examine station-level trends to identify which neighborhoods saw the most growth, or explore demographic shifts using the available subscriber birth year and gender data.
